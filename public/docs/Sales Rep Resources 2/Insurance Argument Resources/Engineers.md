# Engineers

## Overview

Structural and forensic engineers are a critical escalation tool in contested insurance claims. When an adjuster disputes the cause, extent, or scope of damage, an independent engineering report carries authority that a contractor's assessment alone cannot match. Use engineers strategically — their reports change the conversation from opinion to documented fact.

---

## When to Engage an Engineer

- Adjuster attributes damage to wear/maintenance rather than storm event
- Adjuster denies structural damage you can physically observe
- Insurance company sends their own engineer first — counter with yours
- Claim involves deck damage, rafter damage, or structural deflection
- Total loss disputes where insurer argues for repair instead of replacement
- Any claim likely to go to appraisal or litigation
- Hail damage disputed on metal surfaces (gutters, flashing, HVAC units)
- Adjuster uses "cosmetic damage only" language on legitimate functional damage

## How to Use an Engineering Report Effectively

1. Obtain the insurer's engineering report first (request it in writing — they are required to provide it).
2. Review it line by line with your Team Leader. Identify every specific claim they make.
3. Commission your engineer with a clear brief: address each disputed point, not just general observations.
4. Submit the report to the adjuster in writing with a cover letter citing specific policy language.
5. If the adjuster refuses to act on the report, escalate to the insurer's claims manager and reference the Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act for the relevant state.

---

## What to Look for in a Good Engineering Report

A strong engineering report for claim purposes should include:

- Site visit date and weather conditions at time of damage
- Photo documentation of each damage point with measurements
- Reference to the specific storm event (date, NOAA/NWS data)
- Professional opinion on causation (storm vs. pre-existing)
- Reference to applicable building codes and manufacturer standards
- PE stamp and signature
- Clear, plain-language conclusion the adjuster cannot easily dismiss

---

## State Licensing Requirements

- Virginia: Engineer must hold a VA PE license (Board for Architects, Professional Engineers, etc.)
- Maryland: Engineer must hold an MD PE license (Maryland State Board of Professional Engineers)
- Pennsylvania: Engineer must hold a PA PE license (Pennsylvania State Registration Board)

Always verify the engineer's license is current in the state of the claim before submitting their report. An unlicensed report will be dismissed immediately.
